Pollution

What is Pollution?

Pollution is the introduction of contaminants into the natural environment that cause adverse change. Pollution can take the form of any substance or energy. Pollutants, the components of pollution, can be either foreign substances/energies or naturally occurring contaminants. Pollution causes an imbalance in the environment. This imbalance threatened the very survival of all forms of life.
India ranks 66 in the 2021 Environmental Performance Index.

Types of Pollution


  1. Air Pollution

Air Pollution is the addition of undesirable materials into the atmosphere either due to natural phenomenoa or due to human activites, which adversely affect the quality of air and hence, life on earth.

Sources of Air Pollution:

Air Pollution is caused by either natural phenomenoa such as volcanic eruptions or human activites like combustion of fossil fuels. Various sources of air pollution due to human activites are:-

Burning of Fossil Fuels
Burning of Fossil Fuels (coal, petroleum, etc.) produces some poisonous gases like carbon monoxide (CO), sulphur dioxide (SO2), oxides of nitrogen (NOx), methane (CH4) and leaves behind a residue.
Eg - Vehicles, Thermal Power Plants, Industries

Indoor Air Pollution
Indoor Pollutants include perfumes, insecticide sprays and cleansing chemicals. These cause irritation of the eyes, nose and throat, headache, dizziness and fatigue. Such immediate effects are usually short-term and curable.
Agricultural Activites
Activites like harvesting of rice and wheat introduce dust particles into the air. These result in various respiratory problems. Petroleum products, fertilisers, insecticides and other chemicals used by farmers emit fumes into the air and thus pollute it.

Tabacco smoking
Tabacco smoking also is a significant factor that adds to air pollution It is the cause of many respiratory diseases including cancer of the lungs.

Effects of Air Pollution:

Greenhouse Effect
Greenhouse Effect is the phenomenon that makes the earth's atmosphere trap the heat from the sun and prevent it from escaping into the outer space, thereby warming the earth's surface.
Some examples of greenhouse gases are methane (CH4), ozone (O3), nitrous oxide (N2O), chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs).

Some of the consequences of greenhouse effect are:-
  • Less rainfall in the temparate zones and more rainfall in the dry areas of the world.
  • Rise in the sea level.
  • Shrinking of forest cover and expansionof desert area.
  • Increase in floods which can damage the world's coastal wetlands, resulting in loss of aquatics fauna.
  • Causing global warming.

Acid Rain
Acid Rain is the rainwater containing sulphuric acid (H2SO4) and nitric acid (HNO3) along with small amounts of carbonic acid (H2CO3) which are formed from the oxides of sulphur, nitrogen and carbon, present in the air as pollutants.

Harmful effects of acid rain are:-
  • It causes extensive damage to the buildings, statues, bridges and sculptural materials.
  • Vegetation gets destroyed due to the acidification of soil and reduced soil fertility.
  • It affects the aquatic ecosystem by increasing the acidity of water in lakes.
  • It damages trees and plants thereby reducing forest cover.
Ozone Depletion
Over the last few years, it has become clear that the stratospheric ozone layer is thinning out gradually. The extreme thinning out of ozone layer is known as ozone hole.
Various ozone depleting agents are chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), nitric oxide (NO), carbon tetrachloride (CCl4), etc.

Harmful effects of ozone layer depletion are:-
  • The ultraviolet raditions are basically cancer causing agents. Due to increased exposure to ultraviolet rays, the chances of occurence of skin cancer in human beings have increased.
  • It can disturb the temperature balance of the earth.
  • It will affect the food chain, agriculture and forestry.
  • It may result in the weakening of the immune system.

  2. Water Pollution

Water Pollution is the contamination of water by chemical or biological substances. This contaminated water is unfit for drinking and is harmful for the health of animals, plants and aquatic life.

Sources of Water Pollution:

The main sources of water pollution are:-

Domestic Waste
The wastewater resulting from household activites is called sewage. It contains dirt of washed clothes, food waste washed down the drains, chemicals used in soaps and detergents etc.
Industrial Waste
Wastewater from industries is called industrial waste. It contains toxic chemicals including oils, greases, dyes, toxic metals such as lead and cadmium. They make wastewater unfit for human use and for aquatic plants and animals. Oil spills from boats, ships and tankers pollute the water bodies.
Agricultural Waste
Agricultural waste generally contains fertilisers, pesticides and other chemicals. Nitrogen and phosphorus present in fertilisers enter the water bodies, help in the excess growth of algae on the surface, which prevents sunlight and oxygen from reaching aquatic animals and plants.

Effects of Water Pollution:

Oxygen Depletion
Aquatic animals do not survive in wastewater discharged from mlik diaries or sugar mills. Though both milk and suagr are not poisonous, but the decay products of milk and sugar absorb oxygen dissolved in water. So, the aquatic animals die because of oxygen depletion.
Toxicity
The wastewater from industries contains metal compounds and other chemicals. This water is toxic for aquatic animals and humans. The metals get stored in fishes and they are passed on to the human beings who eat these fishes. This proves fatal.
Eutrophication (Algal Bloom)
Eutrophication is the process of increase in the nutrient content of the aquatic ecosystems followed by the excessive growth of algae and weeds on the water surface. Ordinally the presence of alga is good for water bodies because algae produce oxygen by photosynthesis. But this is true only when algae are not present in large amounts.
Eutrophication has the following effects:-
  • The cover of algae does not allow sunlight to reach the aquatic plants. Aquatic plants die in the absence of sunlight.
  • The death of aquatic plants reduces the production of oxygen in water.
  • Algae, on dying, sink down to the bottom. The dead algae decay and consume oxygen, resulting in oxygen depletion.
Water-borne Diseases
Sewage often flows into water bodies. As a result, the microorganisms present in the alt="vehicles"> sewage spread diseases such as typoid and jaundice. Many people get infected from these diseases when they use polluted water.

  3. Land Pollution

Land Pollution refers to all forms of pollution affecting any type of soil: agricultural, forestry, urban, etc. Soil pollution is a disruptive element for many biological resources and ecosystems.

Sources of Land Pollution:

The main sources of land pollution are:-

Mining
Mining is the extraction of valuable minerals or other geological materials from the Earth. It results in erosion, sinkholes, loss of biodiversity, groundwater, and surface water by the chemicals emitted from mining processes.


Intensive Farming
Intensive Farming is a type of farming, both of crop plants and of animals, with higher levels of input and output per unit of agricultural land area. It uses huge amounts of water, energy, and industrial chemicals, increasing pollution in the arable land, usable water, and atmosphere. Herbicides, insecticides, and fertilizers accumulate in ground and surface waters.
Microplastics
Microplastics are fragments of any type of plastic less than 5 mm (0.20 in) in length. They cause land pollution by entering ecosystems from a garbage disposal, industrial processes etc.


Effects of Land Pollution:

On the Environment
Contamination of groundwater, loss of topsoil, water nutrient enrichment and increased risk of wildfires (because of the dry conditions created by the pollutants in the soil) are among the effects on the Environment by land pollution.
On Wildlife
As land pollution and soil erosion progress, animals are forced to shift habitats and adapt to new conditions. As a result, some species are at risk of extinction.
On Human Health
The potential effects of soil contamination on human health include breathing disorders, birth defects, skin diseases, and cancer.








Prevention of Pollution


  • Conserve energy - at home, at work, everywhere.
  • Use public transportation whenever possible.
  • Follow gasoline refueling instructions for efficient vapor recovery, being careful not to spill fuel and always tightening your gas cap securely.
  • Use environmentally safe paints and cleaning products whenever possible.
  • Avoid burning leaves, trash, and other materials.
  • Follow the 4R's policy.
  • Use fans instead of Air Conditioner.
  • Plant more trees.
  • Regular pollution check of the vehicles should be done.
  • Do water harvesting.
